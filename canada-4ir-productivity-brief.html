Intelligence Brief

Innovation & Digital Policy · July 2026

Canada is losing ground in the Fourth Industrial Revolution. The patents say the prize is real.

New firm-level evidence from the OECD shows two things at once. Canadian firms that build core digital technologies become measurably more productive. And Canada's share of exactly that kind of innovation is shrinking, against the same partners it is now diversifying trade toward. With Budget 2025's innovation reforms now law and a patent box still on the table, the evidence arrives at the moment policy is actually moving.

By Jeremy Depow, Principal · RiverRidge Policy & Risk Intelligence · riverridgeintelligence.ca

Page 1 · Executive summary

Bottom line

The OECD's 2025 study of Fourth Industrial Revolution (4IR) patenting is built on the full population of Canadian firms rather than a sample. It finds that when Canadian firms develop core 4IR technologies such as connectivity, software, and IT hardware, their labour and multi-factor productivity improve relative to peers.1 That is the productivity payoff Canada has been chasing for a decade, and it is measurable. A firm that doubles its 4IR patent output is associated with roughly a 0.9 percent gain in multi-factor productivity, 1.4 percent more employment, and 1.7 percent more value added within three years.1 The same study shows Canada's 4IR patenting growing at a third of the global pace and its advantage in core technologies collapsing, while Korea, Sweden, Germany, and the United Kingdom hold or strengthen theirs. Canada is producing less of the innovation the evidence says pays, at the moment it most needs the growth.

The findings, in plain terms:

  • The payoff is real, and specific. Productivity gains come from core technologies. Patents in application domains, meaning smart vehicles, consumer goods, and services, make firms bigger but not more productive. Gains are strongest for smaller firms, and the entry bar into 4IR innovation is lower than for conventional patenting.1
  • Canada's pace is slow and its core advantage is gone. Canadian 4IR patenting grew about 3 percent a year against 8 percent globally. Canada's revealed advantage in core 4IR technologies fell from 1.4 to 0.7 in a decade. Canada now holds under 2 percent of world 4IR patents.1
  • The diversification partners are pulling away. Korea sits among the top four 4IR patenting economies. Sweden out-patents Canada in 4IR with a quarter of the population. The UK and Germany strengthened their specializations over the decade Canada lost its own.1
  • Quality is the asset to build on. Canadian 4IR patents score above world average on originality, radicalness, and citations by later inventions. Canada holds real emerging strengths in smart agriculture, healthcare, 3D systems, and a fast-rising core AI share.1
  • The toolkit is generous, misaligned, and finally moving. Canada ranks near the top of the OECD in indirect R&D support and near the bottom in direct support, an imbalance identified in 2011 and never corrected.5 SR&ED was just reformed, a patent box is promised, and the clusters face credible criticism that they measure activity instead of outcomes.67

What Canada needs to do

  • Tilt support toward core digital technology, where the productivity return is proven, not only toward applications, where firms grow but efficiency does not.
  • Leverage the verified strengths in agri-tech, health technology, AI, and quantum, and build core-technology capacity underneath them so the value stays here.
  • Measure programs on productivity, business R&D, and IP ownership, not project counts. Deliver the promised patent box.
  • Use the trade diversification agenda to pull demand for Canadian core technology, and file patents where the trade policy is going.

The rest of this brief is the detail: what the study found, how Canada compares beyond the United States, why the current programs miss, and what should change.

The detail

01

The productivity problem the patents sit inside

Canada's productivity record frames everything that follows. Labour productivity growth has been among the worst in the G7 and has declined every year since the pandemic except a short 2020 rebound. Multi-factor productivity took one of its largest drops since 1980 in 2021. Business investment fell away after the 2014 oil-price crash and never recovered its trajectory.1

The innovation inputs tell the same story. Business R&D has been flat near 1 percent of GDP for a decade against an OECD average of 2 percent, even though Canadian government support for R&D is above the OECD average. Canada's total R&D intensity has stagnated near 1.7 percent of GDP while the OECD average sits at 2.7 percent, and the World Intellectual Property Organization lists Canada among the economies with the largest declines in share of global R&D since 2000.13 Generous inputs, weak uptake, poor output. That is the standing puzzle, and it is why the OECD's patent study matters. It moves the question from the economy in aggregate to the firms where the decision to innovate actually happens.

The study, published by the OECD in 2025, identifies patents across 21 4IR technology fields. It groups them into core technologies (connectivity, software, IT hardware), enabling technologies (AI, data management, data security, and others), and application domains (smart vehicles, consumer goods, healthcare, agriculture). Its Canadian econometrics run on the full population of firms through confidential Statistics Canada microdata, every firm rather than a commercial sample. That is what makes its findings unusually credible.1

02

The comparison that matters now is not the United States

Canadian innovation debates default to the US comparison. On that axis the story is familiar. The United States gained large 4IR specialization in core and enabling technologies over the decade while Canada lost its own.1 But Canada is deliberately reducing its dependence on the American market. It is deepening CETA with the European Union, trading under the CPTPP with Japan and now the United Kingdom, and holding a bilateral agreement with Korea since 2015. So the relevant question has changed. How does Canada's digital innovation base compare with the partners it is pivoting toward?

The answer from the patent data is uncomfortable. The economies Canada is diversifying into are, almost uniformly, 4IR-strengthening economies.1

Why this matters for diversification is straightforward. Trade agreements set the terms of exchange. The innovation base determines what a country has to exchange. If Canada enters its deepened European, Indo-Pacific, and Korean relationships as a supplier of resources and application-layer products while its partners own the core digital stack, then diversification succeeds on volume and fails on value. That would reproduce with new partners the branch-plant pattern Canada has spent decades regretting with the old one.

The filing data makes the point sharper. Canadian 4IR inventions are protected in only two jurisdictions on average, fewer than almost any other economy, with 37 percent first filed in the United States and 29 percent protected only there.1 Canadian innovators are not filing where Canadian trade policy is going.

Trade agreements set the terms of exchange. The innovation base determines what you have to exchange. Canada is fixing the first while the second erodes.

03

The scorecard: original, radical, concentrated, and losing share

Global 4IR patenting more than doubled between 2007-11 and 2017-21 and now accounts for 19 percent of everything filed at the US patent office. Canada participated at roughly a third of the pace, about 3 percent annual growth against 8 percent globally.1 The sharpest measure is revealed technological advantage, which compares the share of 4IR in a country's patent portfolio with the world's. Canada's core-technology index fell from 1.4 to 0.7, from clearly specialized to clearly not. Enabling technologies held at 1.15 to 1.14. Application domains slipped from 1.3 to 1.18.1

Underneath those totals, old strengths in connectivity, power supply, and consumer goods disappeared. New specializations emerged in agriculture, at an index of 3.5 and among the highest recorded anywhere, along with 3D systems and healthcare. Core AI jumped from 1.4 percent to 21.9 percent of Canada's enabling-technology patents.1

The consolation is calibre. Canadian 4IR patents score above the world average on originality, radicalness, and citations by later inventions.1 Canada's problem is not the quality of what it invents. It is the volume, the drift of the mix away from core technologies, and the direction of travel.

The BlackBerry shadow

One company reshapes how the national numbers should be read. BlackBerry accounts for nearly 30 percent of all 4IR patents filed by Canadian residents between 2000 and 2021. Its filings peaked above 1,200 a year in 2010-12 and fell to roughly 30 by 2021. Nortel, the second name on the list, is gone entirely.1

That cuts two ways. Read one way, the national decline is less broad-based than the curves suggest. Read the other way, it is worse. Canada's 4IR capacity was concentrated enough that one firm's fall moved the national statistics, and nothing of comparable scale replaced it. The rest of the leaderboard, including ATI, TD Bank, Thales Canada, BCE, Geotab, Mitel, RBC, and Shopify, is a long tail of much smaller portfolios.1 The pipeline also points the wrong way. Young Canadian firms patent mostly in application domains, healthcare and agriculture above all, not in the core technologies where the productivity return concentrates.1

04

What 4IR investment actually buys

The econometric core of the paper asks who files 4IR patents and what happens as they file more. Larger and more productive firms are more likely to hold them, in both the Canadian and international samples. But the entry thresholds are lower for 4IR than for other patenting. Firms can get into this game at smaller size and lower productivity than conventional R&D-heavy invention requires.1

The growth payoff, stated plainly

In the full-population Canadian data, a firm that doubles its 4IR patent output is associated with roughly a 0.9 percent gain in multi-factor productivity, a 1.4 percent increase in employment, and a 1.7 percent increase in value added over three years, relative to comparable firms and controlling for its other patenting.1 For a firm of 200 people, that employment figure is about three jobs. Modest on its own, compounding across an economy, and consistent with the federal government's own patent box consultation finding that patent-owning businesses grow faster and pay higher wages.8

The gains are not evenly spread. The productivity improvement is driven entirely by core 4IR technologies: connectivity, software, IT hardware. Application-domain patents are associated with firms scaling up but not becoming more productive. The evidence also suggests smaller firms capture the largest productivity gains, while large firms mainly grow.1 Invention in the plumbing pays differently than invention in the fixtures.

What this evidence cannot tell you

These are firm-level associations among firms that already patent in 4IR. They do not support a national estimate of jobs or GDP, and this brief does not offer one. The study's Canadian microdata was accessed for econometric analysis only, so the number of Canadian 4IR-patenting firms and their total employment are not available to scale from, and a scenario in which every such firm doubles its patenting is not a forecast.1 Readers should treat any headline jobs figure attached to this kind of study with suspicion, including one from us.

Three further cautions belong on the record. Patents are the tip of the innovation iceberg and say nothing about adoption by the firms that never file one. The data ends in 2021, so the entire generative AI wave is invisible and nobody yet knows the shape of these curves after it. And Canada losing advantage partly reflects others surging rather than Canadian activity falling in absolute terms. The authors are explicit that this is correlation, not causation.1

With those limits stated, the pattern holds up. It is population-wide, robust to controls, specific to core technologies, and strongest where theory predicts. It is the best firm-level evidence yet assembled that the Fourth Industrial Revolution's productivity gains have begun to materialize for the Canadian firms that participate in building it.

05

The programs: generous, misaligned, and finally moving

Canada does not lack innovation programs. It lacks alignment between what the programs reward and what the evidence says pays. Three files show it.

SR&ED, and the report that was half-implemented

The Scientific Research and Experimental Development credit delivers over 4.2 billion dollars a year and is the backbone of Canadian R&D support.6 The effectiveness question is not whether it funds research. It is whether it moves the needle. Through a decade of generous SR&ED, business R&D sat flat at 1 percent of GDP while most OECD countries rose.1

This is not a new diagnosis. In 2011 the Jenkins panel, formally Innovation Canada: A Call to Action, examined 60 programs worth about 5 billion dollars and found federal support was overweighted toward subsidizing the cost of R&D rather than the other things innovative firms need. Roughly 70 percent of the money flowed through SR&ED and 30 percent through 59 direct programs. Its central recommendation was to rebalance: narrow SR&ED for small firms to labour costs and redeploy the savings into direct support that helps firms grow.5

What happened next is the part worth knowing. Budget 2012 took the narrowing and skipped the redeployment. The general credit rate dropped from 20 to 15 percent, capital expenditures were removed, and the overhead proxy was trimmed. The proposed Industrial Research and Innovation Council was never created. The imbalance the panel identified survived: Statistics Canada's own review records Canada ranking 4th of 38 countries in indirect government support for business R&D and 28th in direct funding.5 And Budget 2025 has now restored capital expenditure eligibility, reversing the one piece of Jenkins that was implemented.6 Fifteen years on, the diagnosis stands and the prescription was taken backwards.

Distribution is the other concern. Reporting drawing on program data indicates small firms receive only about 20 percent of the total SR&ED benefit, with much of the rest going to a small number of large claimants, and over 20 percent flowing to foreign firms performing R&D in Canada.5 Set against the OECD finding that productivity gains are largest for small firms, that allocation is pointed away from where the return is.

Budget 2025 delivered the largest SR&ED reform in a generation, and it is now law. The enhanced 35 percent credit's expenditure limit doubled to 6 million dollars, phase-out thresholds rose to a 15 to 75 million dollar range, eligibility extended to Canadian public corporations, and capital expenditures returned. From April 2026 the CRA adds elective pre-claim approval, 90-day processing for approved claims, and simplified forms.6 Pre-claim approval in particular answers a real complaint, since firms previously learned whether a project qualified only after spending the money. These are genuine improvements to generosity and predictability. What they do not do is steer the subsidy toward the kind of R&D with the demonstrated productivity return. SR&ED remains technology-neutral by design. The OECD evidence suggests neutrality has a cost when the payoff is concentrated in core digital technologies.

The clusters: measuring activity, not outcomes

The Global Innovation Clusters, the renamed superclusters, carry roughly 2 billion dollars in federal commitments. The criticism record is substantial and specific. The Auditor General found the program's founding claim of a fifty-fold return had no analytical basis and no plan to measure it. The Parliamentary Budget Officer found spending far behind schedule in the early years, roughly 30 million dollars against an anticipated 104 million. Innovation scholars, including the University of Toronto's Innovation Policy Lab, argue the program's indicators, meaning project counts, co-investment totals, and partnership numbers, measure activity rather than what matters: productivity growth, firms' sales and exports, and whether business R&D actually rises.7

The Council of Canadian Innovators' critique lands closest to this brief's evidence. Its chair argued the program's design was flawed because it evaluated IP assets by counting them rather than asking whether Canadian companies had secured strategic IP rights that let them operate in the world.7 Set against the OECD findings, the design flaw is exact. This is a program built to generate collaborative projects in application domains, in an economy whose productivity problem lives in core technologies, measured by indicators that cannot detect the difference.

The patent box and the IP agenda: promised, pending

The most consequential open file is the patent box, a reduced tax rate on income from Canadian-held IP. It is designed to fix the pattern the OECD data documents: Canadian inventions filed primarily in the US and commercialized elsewhere. The Commons science committee recommended it, federal consultations ran in 2024, Budget 2025 stated the intent to implement one, and details remain pending as of mid-2026. British Columbia is meanwhile running its own patent box consultation for the life sciences sector, open to 24 June 2026.89

Alongside it, Budget 2025 renewed the SME-facing IP toolkit with ElevateIP at 84.4 million dollars over four years, the Innovation Asset Collective's Patent Collective at 22.5 million over three, and the NRC's IP Assist at 75 million over three. It committed to a national Intellectual Property Performance Review, and introduced a Productivity Super-Deduction allowing immediate expensing of patents, data network infrastructure, and computers.6 The direction is right. The test is whether the patent box arrives with substance requirements that reward IP developed and held in Canada, and whether the Performance Review measures ownership and productivity rather than filing counts.

06

Recommendations

1. Tilt support toward core digital technology. The productivity return concentrates in connectivity, software, and IT hardware. SR&ED's new generosity, the clusters' next funding phase, and the Strategic Innovation Fund should carry an explicit core-4IR lens. A smart-consumer-goods project and a network-infrastructure project are not equivalent, and the evidence says so.

2. Build on the verified strengths rather than lamenting the lost ones. The data shows where Canada is strong and getting stronger: smart agriculture at a revealed advantage of 3.5, health technology, 3D systems, a core AI share that jumped from 1.4 to 21.9 percent of enabling patents, and a quantum ecosystem Ottawa is now backing with 334 million dollars through the Defence Industrial Strategy.16 The play is to build core-technology capacity underneath these strengths, meaning the data systems, connectivity, and software infrastructure of agri-tech and health-tech, so the productivity-bearing layer of those value chains is Canadian too.

3. Fix what programs measure. Adopt the standard the innovation scholars have been asking for. Evaluate the clusters and the major funds on productivity growth, business R&D intensity, firm sales and exports, and Canadian IP ownership, not on project and partnership counts. What gets measured is what program managers optimize.

4. Finish Jenkins, or say plainly why not. The 2011 rebalance from indirect to direct support was never completed, and Canada still sits 4th in indirect support and 28th in direct.5 Either redeploy toward direct, targeted support for growing firms as the panel recommended, or make the case that fifteen years of evidence has changed the answer. Doing neither is how a diagnosis becomes a habit.

5. Deliver the patent box with teeth. Announce the design, include substance requirements tying the preferential rate to R&D performed and IP held in Canada, and pair it with the promised IP-backed financing framework so young firms can borrow against the patents the regime encourages them to keep.

6. Point trade diversification at 4IR demand. CETA, the CPTPP, and bilateral technology partnerships should be worked as market-creation instruments for Canadian core technology, through digital trade chapters, mutual recognition, procurement access, and standards cooperation. Filing strategy should follow. Two-jurisdiction protection is not a diversification posture.

7. Solve for scale, not just start-ups. The BlackBerry lesson is that Canada's 4IR base lacked redundancy at the top. The Budget 2025 capital measures, the billion-dollar Venture and Growth Capital Catalyst and the forthcoming 750-million-dollar growth-stage strategy, are aimed at the right gap.6 Their test is whether they keep scaling firms, and their IP, in Canada.

07

Why now

This evidence lands in an active policy moment. Budget 2025, tabled in November 2025, made innovation, AI, and IP its economic centrepiece, and its SR&ED reforms have received Royal Assent. The federal patent box decision is pending while British Columbia consults on its own. A national Intellectual Property Performance Review is being launched. A Minister of Artificial Intelligence and Digital Innovation now exists, Statistics Canada is standing up a program to measure AI's economic impact, and the CRA's overhauled SR&ED administration takes effect in April 2026.69

The question this brief raises is whether the newly generous toolkit will point at the technologies where the productivity evidence says the return actually is. That will be answered in the design details of these files over the coming year. It is the right year to be asking.

The shape of the argument is simple. The technologies of the Fourth Industrial Revolution are not a productivity promise waiting to be redeemed someday. For the firms building their core, in the only full-population dataset we have, the returns have begun. Canada's problem is that fewer of its firms are building them, in a mix drifting toward the lower-payoff end, on a base that never replaced its fallen champions, while the partners it is diversifying toward pull ahead. That is a more specific problem than productivity, and specific problems are the ones policy can aim at.
